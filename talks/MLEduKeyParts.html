What is the main difficulty?

What do you need to succeed?

  • Assemble a strong teaching team with both academic and industry experts
  • Thought out and resolved yourself assignments
  • Trust and openness, chat with students
  • Kaggle-style competitions
  • Better to record and quickly share video lectures
  • Interactive and emotional atmosphere, questions for the audience
And how do you think, what is the main difficulty in teaching machine learning? It seems to me that we NEED to combine math theory and programming practice, and this is not an easy task.

To ensure success, the following STEPS are crucial:
1. You NEED to assemble a strong teaching team with both academic and industry experts to ensure students receive high-quality education and practical insights.
2. It IS crucial to have well-thought and resolved assignments. This is essential for mastering machine learning concepts.
3. You NEED to establish trust and openness with students by engaging in regular conversations and addressing their concerns.
4. We NEED to create a competitive learning environment, for example, by incorporating contests like Kaggle competitions to motivate students.
5. Nowadays, we MUST record and promptly upload lectures to accommodate different learning paces and ensure students who miss classes can catch up.
6. We NEED to foster an interactive and emotional atmosphere, even in online settings, by asking questions and encouraging participation.
In conclusion, it's important to maintain an open and accessible teaching environment.

How do we teach Machine Learning at Neapolis University?

Part 1: Fundamentals of Machine Learning

  • ML tasks types, examples, quality evaluation
  • Linear models, gradient descent, decision trees
  • Ensembles, random forest, and gradient boosting
  • Fully connected neural networks and backpropagation
  • Building GPT-2 from scratch

Part 2: Advanced Machine Learning

  • Clustering and EM-algorithm
  • LLM: tokenization, interpretations, embeddings
  • Building AlphaZero from scratch
  • Generative and discriminative models: VAE, GANs, flows, and diffusion
  • RAGs and multi agents LLMs

And how to address the question about course updates?

In our Machine Learning course at Neapolis University students write their code for different machine learning algorithms to understand the inner details deeply. The course also includes theoretical and practical tasks and culminates in an exam consisting of six problems for three hours.

The whole one-year course consists of two parts. The FIRST part covers the fundamentals of machine learning, including linear models, decision trees, ensembles, neural networks, and even building GPT-2 from scratch.

The SECOND part delves into advanced machine learning topics like clustering, tokenization, building AlphaZero from scratch, and generative and discriminative models like Variational Auto Encoders, Generative Adversarial Networks, and Retrieval Augmented Generation.

To address the question about course UPDATES: indeed, each year, the course needs to be revised by at least one third. While it might be possible to retain the same content in the second year, by the third year, significant changes are almost always necessary.
Our course structure is limited to 13 lectures, so adding new material means removing some of the old. This limitation helps us maintain a manageable course size.

Assignments are the crucial part of the course, and we have a variety of them.

In the FIRST part, we have about six or seven assignments. The first one is about mastering NumPy and Pandas. Then we move on to the theory of linear models and studying ML with practical projects.
Also, we have two competitions as a valuable tool for motivation. Public leaderboards are used to encourage a healthy competitive spirit without discouraging students.

In the SECOND part of the course, students have four assignments and a final project. We try to make the assignments as practical and as up-to-date as possible. So students can apply their knowledge to real-world problems, such as implementing a minimal version of LLama or creating your own voice assistant Jarvis to talk with on your own laptop. At the end of the course, students present their final project and write a blog post about it. It is also an important part of education, which can be used as an advertisement for the student.

To address the issue of assessing criteria: at the beginning of a course we set clear and fixed points for each assignment in advance. Generally, achieving a passing grade is relatively straightforward, requiring about HALF of the total points. This ensures that students who complete the assignments diligently, can pass, while higher grades require more comprehensive work. Sometimes we need to slightly adjust our grading system at the end of the course.
