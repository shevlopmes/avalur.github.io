Creating Machine Learning course from scratch

I have highlighted some of the key aspects that are most clear and understandable for us. The rapid development we see in many fields is also happening in machine learning. If you take a modern course in biology, for example, you'll see that about 30% of the material changes each year because science and progress keep advancing.
So we need to provide students with the most up-to-date knowledge, which means frequently updating our courses. For sure this is also true for machine learning.
Last year, I had the opportunity to develop a new machine learning course from scratch at Neapolis University. I will talk about this concrete course, and then we discuss what is happening with teaching ML in general.

One of the most popular courses is Andrew Ng's course on Coursera. Many modern machine learning educators and researchers have grown up on this course.

Another course I reviewed is the Open Data Science community's course, which is available on GitHub. It includes well-crafted lecture notes, theory descriptions in PDF format, and practical assignments. Everything is available for free, and nowadays, this is a common practice.
Of course, there are many other resources available, like the interactive textbook "Dive into Deep Learning" by the big team of authors, mostly working at Amazon. Also you can watch video courses on YouTube with some materials. Such resources allow us to build a comprehensive course using a variety of materials.
Moreover, we cannot only use the available materials I mentioned as a basis for our own course, but we can and even should use AI tools to improve our materials: for example, to make easier addition of practical tasks and tests to them.

To ensure success, the following steps are crucial:
1. Assemble a strong teaching team with both academic and industry experts to ensure students receive high-quality education and practical insights.
2. Crucial to have well-thought-out and resolved assignments. This is essential for mastering machine learning concepts. Students must practice coding and theoretical problems to reinforce their learning.
3. Establish trust and openness with students by engaging in regular conversations and addressing their concerns.
4. We need to create a competitive learning environment by incorporating contests like Kaggle competitions to motivate students.
5. Nowaday we must record and promptly upload lectures to accommodate different learning paces and ensure students who miss classes can catch up.
6. Foster an interactive and emotional learning atmosphere, even in online settings, by asking questions and encouraging participation.
In conclusion, it's important to maintain an open and accessible teaching environment, leveraging modern tools and methods to engage students effectively. All the materials for my courses are also available on GitHub under an open MIT license.

How do they teach Machine Learning at Neapolis University Pafos?

Part 1: Fundamentals of Machine Learning

  • ML tasks types, examples, quality evaluation
  • Linear models, gradient descent, decision trees
  • Ensembles, random forest and gradient boosting
  • Fully-connected neural networks and backpropagation
  • Building GPT-2 from scratch

Part 2: Advanced Machine Learning

  • Clustering and EM-algorithm
  • LLM: tokenization, interpretations, embeddings
  • Building AlphaZero from scratch
  • Generative and discriminative models: VAE, GANs, flows and diffusion
  • RAGs and multi agents LLMs
For example in our Machine Learning course at Neapolis University students write their code for algorithms like linear regression and decision trees to understand the inner details deeply.
The course also includes theoretical and practical tasks and culminates in an exam consisting of a 30-40 minute interview to assess the students' understanding.
One-year course consists of two parts. The first part covers the fundamentals of machine learning, including linear models, decision trees, ensembles, neural networks, and building GPT-2 from scratch.

The second part delves into advanced machine learning topics like clustering, tokenization, building AlphaZero from scratch, and generative and discriminative models like VAE, GANs, and RAGs.

To address the question about course updates: indeed, each year, the course needs to be revised by at least 30%. This is challenging for educators who recently developed the course and would prefer to reuse much of the content. While it might be possible to retain the same content in the second year, by the third year, significant changes are necessary.
To manage this, our team regularly reviews and updates the course content. We assess which lectures need modification, which should be removed, and which new material should be added. This can be painful, especially when removing well-prepared materials, but it's a necessary part of keeping the course up-to-date. The speed of development in machine learning doesn't seem to be slowing down, and it might continue at this pace for the long time. Our course structure is limited to 13 lectures, so adding new material means removing some of the old. This limitation helps us maintain a manageable course size.

Assignments

  • Mastering NumPy and Pandas
  • Theory of linear models
  • Machine Learning 101 via projects
  • Table binary classification on Kaggle
  • Two-layer neural network from scratch in numpy
  • Image classification on Kaggle
  • Implement clustering and EM-algorithm
  • Implement minLLama
  • Build AlphaZero from scratch
  • Build Your Own Jarvis
  • Final project with blogpost
Assignments are a crucial part of the course, and we have a variety of them.
To address the issue of assessing new assignments and criteria at the beginning of a course: we set clear and fixed points for each assignment in advance. While the criteria for getting specific grades might be adjusted based on initial feedback and performance, the overall structure remains constant. Generally, achieving a passing grade is relatively straightforward, requiring about half of the total points. This ensures that students who complete the assignments diligently can pass, while higher grades require more comprehensive work.
Competitions are a valuable tool for motivation but can demotivate those at the bottom of the leaderboard. To mitigate this, we make competition part optional and provide private feedback on individual standings. Public leaderboards are used cautiously to encourage a healthy competitive spirit without discouraging students.

In the second part of the course students have four assignments and final project. We try to make the assignments as practical and as up-to-date as possible, so students can apply their knowledge to real-world problems, such as implementing minLLama or creating your own voice assistant Jarvis to talk with. In the end of the course, students present their final project and write a blog post about it.

Setup example for online teaching in 2024

  • Slides in JavaScript (reveal.js): for animations, video and interactivity
  • Formulas and hand drew pictures in Microsoft Whiteboard on iPad
  • PyCharm and Jupyter notebooks for coding demos
  • OBS Studio for combining different scenes and streaming it
Now I'd like to share with you an effective setup for online teaching in 2024. Let's go through the components that can enhance our teaching experience.

First, I used to make slides in JavaScript using Reveal.js framework. This tool is great for creating dynamic and interactive presentations. With Reveal.js, you can incorporate animations, embed videos, and add interactivity to keep students engaged.

Next, for formulas and hand-drawn pictures, I use Microsoft Whiteboard on iPad. This combination allows for real-time, intuitive drawing and writing, which is particularly useful for explaining complex concepts and equations in a visually accessible manner.

For coding demonstrations, I prefer turn to PyCharm and Jupyter Notebooks. PyCharm is an excellent IDE for Python, offering robust coding support and debugging tools. Jupyter Notebooks are perfect for interactive coding sessions, where you can show live code execution and visualizations.

Finally, to bring everything together you may use Open Broadcast Software Studio. It is a powerful tool for combining different scenes, such as your slides, whiteboard, and coding environment, into a seamless streaming experience.
This setup not only enhances the visual quality of your lectures but also allows you to switch between different teaching materials smoothly.
By integrating these tools, we can create a comprehensive and engaging online teaching environment that fits various learning styles and keeps our students actively involved.

And what about teaching routine automatization?

Unfortunately, I have bad news for you =) Despite the rapid development of AI assistants and systems, such as ChatGPT, which now are capable of generating text, code, and are irreplaceable in many areas, the teaching routine is not yet automated.
  • AI can help you create a draft of one slide or transform a short course description into a nice-looking syllabus
  • But the key content of the course is up to you
  • It cannot create interesting, well-thought-out, and detailed homework assignments
  • It cannot check the students' code and provide feedback
  • Maybe the only way of using such technology in teaching is to create a chatbot that will answer the most common questions from students


So it will not replace a real teacher, but it can help to save some of your time

AI can help you create a draft of one slide or transform a short description of your course into a nice looking syllabus, but the key content of the course is up to you.
It cannot create interesting, well-thought-out and detailed homework assignments. It even cannot check the students' code and provide feedback.
Maybe the only way of using such technology in teaching is to create a chatbot that will answer the most common questions from students. Of course, it will not replace a real teacher, but it can help to save your time.
