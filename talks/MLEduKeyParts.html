Creating Machine Learning course from scratch

Creating Machine Learning course from scratch is a challenging task. I tried to highlight some of the key aspects that are most clear and understandable for us. The rapid development we see in many fields is also happening in machine learning. If you take a modern course in biology, for example, you'll see that about one third of the material changes each year because science and progress keep advancing.
So we need to provide students with the most up-to-date knowledge, which means frequently updating our courses. For sure, this is also true for machine learning.
Last year, I had the opportunity to develop a new machine learning course from scratch at Neapolis University. I will talk mostly about this course, but suggest starting with a short intro.
One of the MOST popular courses in ML is Andrew Ng's course on Coursera. Many modern machine learning educators and researchers have grown up on this course.

ANOTHER course I know is the Open Data Science community's course, which is available on GitHub. It includes well-crafted lecture notes, theory descriptions in PDF format, and practical assignments. Everything is available for free, and nowadays, this is a common practice.
Of course, there are many other resources available, like the interactive TEXTBOOK "Dive into Deep Learning" by the big team of authors, mostly working at an Amazon company. Also, you can watch and code with VIDEO courses on YouTube. Such resources allow us to build a comprehensive course using a variety of materials.

What is the main difficulty?

What do you need to succeed?

  • Assemble a strong teaching team with both academic and industry experts
  • Thought out and resolved yourself assignments
  • Trust and openness, chat with students
  • Kaggle-style competitions
  • Better to record and quickly share video lectures
  • Interactive and emotional atmosphere, questions for the audience
And how do you think, what is the main difficulty in teaching machine learning? It seems to me that we NEED to combine math theory and programming practice, and this is not easy.

To ensure success, the following STEPS are crucial:
1. You NEED to assemble a strong teaching team with both academic and industry experts to ensure students receive high-quality education and practical insights.
2. It IS crucial to have well-thought and resolved assignments. This is essential for mastering machine learning concepts. Students must practice coding and theoretical problems to reinforce their learning.
3. You NEED to establish trust and openness with students by engaging in regular conversations and addressing their concerns.
4. We NEED to create a competitive learning environment by incorporating contests like Kaggle competitions to motivate students.
5. Nowaday we MUST record and promptly upload lectures to accommodate different learning paces and ensure students who miss classes can catch up.
6. We NEED to foster an interactive and emotional learning atmosphere, even in online settings, by asking questions and encouraging participation.
In conclusion, it's important to maintain an open and accessible teaching environment.

How do we teach Machine Learning at Neapolis University?

Part 1: Fundamentals of Machine Learning

  • ML tasks types, examples, quality evaluation
  • Linear models, gradient descent, decision trees
  • Ensembles, random forest and gradient boosting
  • Fully connected neural networks and backpropagation
  • Building GPT-2 from scratch

Part 2: Advanced Machine Learning

  • Clustering and EM-algorithm
  • LLM: tokenization, interpretations, embeddings
  • Building AlphaZero from scratch
  • Generative and discriminative models: VAE, GANs, flows and diffusion
  • RAGs and multi agents LLMs
In our Machine Learning course at Neapolis University students write their code for different ML algorithms to understand the inner details deeply. The course also includes theoretical and practical tasks and culminates in an exam consisting of six problems for three hours to assess the students' understanding.

One-year course consists of two parts. The FIRST part covers the fundamentals of machine learning, including linear models, decision trees, ensembles, neural networks, and building GPT-2 from scratch.

The SECOND part delves into advanced machine learning topics like clustering, tokenization, building AlphaZero from scratch, and generative and discriminative models like Variational Auto Encoders, Generative Adversarial Networks, and Retrival Augmented Generation.

To address the question about course updates: indeed, each year, the course needs to be revised by at least one third. This is challenging for educators who recently developed the course and would prefer to reuse much of the content. While it might be possible to retain the same content in the second year, by the third year, significant changes are almost always necessary.
To manage this, we assess which lectures need modification, which should be removed, and which new material should be added. Our course structure is limited to 13 lectures, so adding new material means removing some of the old. This limitation helps us maintain a manageable course size.

Assignments are a crucial part of the course, and we have a variety of them.
To address the issue of assessing criteria at the beginning of a course: we set clear and fixed points for each assignment in advance. While the criteria for getting specific grades might be adjusted based on initial feedback and performance, the overall structure remains constant. Generally, achieving a passing grade is relatively straightforward, requiring about half of the total points. This ensures that students who complete the assignments diligently can pass, while higher grades require more comprehensive work.

In the FIRST part, we have about six or seven assignments. The first one is about mastering NumPy and Pandas. Then we move on to the theory of linear models and studying ML via practical projects.
Also, we have two competitions as a valuable tool for motivation. Public leaderboards are used cautiously to encourage a healthy competitive spirit without discouraging students.

In the SECOND part of the course, students have four assignments and final project. We try to make the assignments as practical and as up to date as possible, so students can apply their knowledge to real-world problems, such as implementing a minimal version of LLama or creating your own voice assistant Jarvis to talk with. At the end of the course, students present their final project and write a blog post about it.

My setup for online teaching in 2024

  • Slides in JavaScript (reveal.js): for animations, video and interactivity
  • Formulas and hand drew pictures in Notability on iPad
  • PyCharm and Jupyter notebooks for coding demos


OBS Studio for combining different scenes and streaming it

Now I'd like to share with you my own setup for online teaching in 2024. Let's go through components step by step.

FIRST, as you see now, I used to make slides in JavaScript using Reveal.js framework. This tool is great for creating dynamic and interactive presentations. With Reveal.js, you can incorporate animations, embed videos, and add interactivity to keep students engaged.

NEXT, for formulas and hand-drawn pictures, I use Notability app on iPad. This combination allows for real-time, intuitive drawing and writing, which is particularly useful for explaining complex concepts and equations in a visually accessible manner.

For CODING demonstrations, I prefer to turn to PyCharm and Jupyter Notebooks. PyCharm is an excellent IDE for Python, offering robust coding support and debugging tools. Jupyter Notebooks are perfect for interactive coding sessions, where you can show live code execution and visualizations.

FINALLY, to bring everything together, you may use Open Broadcast Software Studio. It is a powerful tool for combining different scenes, such as your slides, whiteboard, and coding environment, into a seamless streaming experience.
This setup not only enhances the visual quality of your lectures but also allows you to switch between different teaching materials smoothly.
Now let me show how it works in practice. You see, I can switch between slides and whiteboard with one click.
Then I can explain the formula of one linear neuron in real time. Also, I can show and comment, adding hand-drawn pictures, the matrix multiplication method and so on. Any complex topic can be explained naturally in this way. And of course I can switch to PyCharm with any code and show how to implement everything in Python from scratch.

And what about teaching routine automatization?

Ok, let's return to slides. You may ask what about teaching routine automatization? Unfortunately, I have bad news for you =)
Despite the rapid development of AI assistants and systems, which now are capable of generating text, code, and are irreplaceable in many areas, the teaching routine is not yet fully automated.
  • AI can help you create a draft of one slide or transform a short course description into a nice-looking syllabus
  • But the key content of the course is up to you
  • It cannot create interesting, well-thought-out, and detailed homework assignments
  • It cannot check the students' code and provide feedback
  • Maybe the only way of using such technology in teaching is to create a chatbot that will answer the most common questions from students


So it will not replace a real teacher, but it can help to save some of your time
